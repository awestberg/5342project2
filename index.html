Phoenician Aleph • 800 BC

The Phoenician alphabet contains 22 letters, all of which are consonants. Phoenician writing ran from right to left; thus, the aleph led with its point.

Greek Alpha• 740 BC

Alpha is the first letter of the Greek alphabet and was derived from the Phoenician letter aleph that also lies on its side. The alphabet has 24 letters, ordered from alpha to omega.

Roman • 100 BC

Like most of our capitals, A reached its perfected form in Roman marble-carved inscriptions of the first century B.C. to second century A.D.

Uncial • 300 AD

Uncial is a majuscule script written entirely in capital letters commonly used from the 3rd to 8th centuries AD by Latin and Greek scribes.

Half Uncial • 800 AD

Half-unicals were written between four guidelines allowing for the development of ascenders and descenders. This new style was easier and faster to write than the uncial style.

Caroline • 1100 AD

Carolingian minuscule was uniform, with rounded shapes in clearly distinguishable glyphs, disciplined and above all, legible. Clear capital letters and spaces between words became standard.

Gothic • 1400 AD

Carolingian, though legible, was time-consuming and labour-intensive to produce. Gothic could be written more quickly and more condensed to meet the growing demand for new books.
